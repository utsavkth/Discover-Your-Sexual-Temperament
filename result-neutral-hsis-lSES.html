Your Sexual Temperament Summary

Profile: High Inhibition (SIS) + Low Excitation (SES)

Hey friend, your sexual system is cautious, quiet, and deeply sensitive. You don’t feel arousal easily, and when you do, it’s fragile — easily interrupted by pressure, shame, or even the slightest emotional misalignment.

💡 What This Looks Like in Real Life

You might not feel desire often. You may go long stretches without it, or only notice it in very specific emotional contexts — and even then, a small disruption (stress, awkwardness, performance pressure) can make it vanish.

This might’ve made you question your identity or wonder if you’re “normal.” But here’s the truth: your system isn’t broken. It’s just cautious. And in a world that glorifies intensity and urgency, you move at the speed of safety — and that’s brave.

🌈 For Those Who Live Outside the Binary

If you identify as non-binary, genderfluid, agender, or anywhere beyond the binary, you may feel doubly disconnected from how sexuality is often portrayed. This profile reminds you: your experience is valid. You don’t have to prove your sexuality. You get to define it.

Whether your brakes are shaped by trauma, identity invalidation, dysphoria, or just the noise of the world — your body is trying to protect you. Let’s work with that, not against it.

✅ Tools That Gently Support Your System

  • 🛏️ Let slowness be sacred: take your time. Warmth, eye contact, quiet — you might need much more of these than you’ve been told is “normal.”
  • 📓 Keep a private, judgment-free arousal log: notice what moments helped you feel even slightly connected to desire — even non-sexual ones like emotional safety or soft touch.
  • 🧠 Understand your brakes: try writing down what shuts you down. External? Internal? Even the awareness is a step forward.
  • 🪷 Explore sensuality without expectation: try bath rituals, soft textures, movement, music — arousal doesn’t always mean “sex.”
  • 💬 Speak your needs unapologetically: if something doesn’t feel right, you don’t have to explain why. “No” is a complete sentence — and your body gets to lead.
🔍 What do SIS and SES actually mean?
